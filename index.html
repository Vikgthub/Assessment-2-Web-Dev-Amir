SOLAR SYSTEM

EXPLORING THE COSMOS

THE SOLAR SYSTEM

The Solar System is the gravitationally bound system of the Sun, consisting of eight planets (Mercury, Venus, Earth, Mars, Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, Neptune), their moons, dwarf planets (such as Pluto), asteroids, and comets, all revolving around the Sun in the Milky Way galaxy. Created approximately 4.6 billion years ago from a collapsing nebula, it primarily consists of empty space controlled by the Sun's mass, with other objects orbiting in elliptical paths.

DISTANCE: 2.5M LY • TYPE: SOLAR

Sun

Sol

The Sun is the central star of our solar system, a gigantic sphere of hot plasma (hydrogen and helium) where nuclear fusion generates the light and warmth necessary for life on Earth, maintaining the system's structure with its powerful gravity, and affecting weather, currents, and climate. It is a G-type yellow dwarf star of middle age, approximately 4.6 billion years old, and the nearest star to Earth, with its energy traveling to us in roughly 8 minutes.

Radius : 696,000 kilometers

Mercury

Closest planet to sun

Mercury, the tiniest planet in our solar system, orbits nearest to the Sun, named after the quick Roman messenger deity, and recognized for its cratered, lunar-like terrain, rapid orbit (88 Earth days), and drastic temperature fluctuations between blistering days and frigid nights, despite possessing a large iron core that gives it considerable density.

DISTANCE (FROM SUN): 69.813 million km

Radius : 2,440 kilometers

Venus

Brightest Planet

Venus, often called Earth's "sister planet," is the second planet from the Sun, recognized for its incredibly hot, poisonous atmosphere, immense pressure, and sluggish, reverse rotation, which causes it to be the hottest planet in our solar system, even though it isn't the nearest to the Sun, as a runaway greenhouse effect from its thick carbon dioxide clouds traps heat and results in a bright, yellowish-white look in the sky.

DISTANCE (FROM SUN): 108 million kilometers

Radius : 6,052 kilometers

Earth

Home

Earth is our planet, the third closest to the Sun, and the only known location in the universe that supports life, due to its liquid water, stable temperatures, and shielding atmosphere. It's a terrestrial planet with a core, mantle, and crust, having varied terrain, large seas, and a moon, created roughly 4.5 billion years back and circling the Sun in approximately 365 days.

DISTANCE (FROM SUN): 150 million kilometers (1 Astronimical Unit)

Radius : 6,378 km

Moon

Light in the Night Sky

The Moon is the sole natural satellite of Earth and ranks as the fifth-largest moon in the Solar System. It is a barren, lifeless planet that revolves around Earth at an average distance of roughly 384,400 km (238,855 miles).

DISTANCE (FROM SUN): 146.7 million km to 152.5 million km

Radius : 1,737 kilometers

Mars

The Red Planet

Mars, the fourth planet from the Sun, is a frigid, rocky, desert-like world referred to as the "Red Planet" because of iron oxide (rust) in its soil, possessing a thin carbon dioxide atmosphere, polar ice caps, volcanoes, canyons, and seasons, with robotic missions currently investigating for evidence of past water and life, while future human missions are being planned.

DISTANCE (FROM SUN): 228 million kilometers

Radius : 3,390 kilometers

Asteroid Belt

The asteroid belt is a doughnut-shaped area located between Mars and Jupiter, populated by millions of rocky objects (asteroids) orbiting the Sun, remnants from the early solar system that failed to form a planet, with Ceres being the largest among them. It is an expansive, largely vacant area filled with various substances, examined to comprehend planet creation and possibly for resource harvesting, although its immensity makes spacecraft collisions uncommon.

DISTANCE (FROM SUN): 329 to 479 million kilometers

Jupiter

Biggest Planet

Jupiter is the fifth planet and the largest in our solar system, a huge gas giant primarily made of hydrogen and helium, recognized for its unique bands, intense storms (such as the Great Red Spot), and many moons, including the Galilean moons (Io, Europa, Ganymede, Callisto). It features fast rotation (brief days), a powerful magnetic field, and lacks a solid surface, with gas layers changing to metallic hydrogen at great depths

DISTANCE (FROM SUN): 778 million kilometers

Radius : 71,492 kilometers

Saturn

Ring Planet

Saturn is the sixth planet from the Sun, a giant gas planet famous for its stunning system of icy rings, second in size to Jupiter, primarily made of hydrogen and helium, and featuring numerous moons such as Titan, rendering it an intriguing giant without a solid surface for landing

DISTANCE (FROM SUN): 1.4 billion kilometers

Radius : 58,232 kilometers

Uranus

Georgium Sidus (George's Star)

Uranus is the seventh planet from the Sun, an ice giant notable for its blue-green color due to methane, severe axial tilt that causes it to turn on its side, and being the coldest planet in the solar system. It has weak rings, 27 known moons (including Titania, Oberon, Ariel, Umbriel, and Miranda), and is primarily made up of frozen fluids (water, methane, and ammonia) over a rocky core, with a hydrogen–helium atmosphere. Its peculiar tilt results in severe seasons, with poles facing the sun for decades.

DISTANCE (FROM SUN): 2.9 billion kilometers

Radius : 25,362 kilometers

Neptune

(Not) Ocean World

Neptune is the eighth and farthest planet from the Sun, a "ice giant" famed for its deep blue color, supersonic winds, and severe storms. It is named after the Roman sea god. It's similar in size to Uranus but larger, with a rocky core surrounded by a fluid mantle of water, ammonia, and methane, and a thick hydrogen, helium, and methane atmosphere. It contains a weak ring system and at least 14 moons, the largest of which, Triton, orbits in retrograde.

DISTANCE (FROM SUN): 4.5 billion km

Radius : 24,622 kilometers

Pluto

The Dwarf Planet

Pluto is a well-known dwarf planet in the Kuiper Belt that was reclassified by the International Astronomical Union (IAU) in 2006 because it does not meet the criteria of "cleaning its orbit" of other objects, as the eight major planets do. It was discovered in 1930 and named after the Roman god of the underworld. It is a small, cold world with a thin atmosphere and five moons (Charon, Nix, Hydra, Kerberos, Styx). In 2015, NASA's New Horizons probe captured spectacular close-up photos of complex features such as mountains and plains.

DISTANCE (FROM SUN): 5.9 billion km

Radius : 1,188 kilometers

Kuiper Belt

The Kuiper Belt is a large, doughnut-shaped ring of frozen bodies (Kuiper Belt Objects, or KBOs) in the outer Solar System that stretches from beyond Neptune's orbit (about 30 Astronomical Units, or AU) to about 50 AU, perhaps much further. It is a leftover from the solar system's formation, similar to the asteroid belt but much larger, and is home to dwarf planets such as Pluto, Eris, Haumea, and Makemake, as well as a significant number of comets. Neptune's gravity has a profound influence on its structure, resulting in various groups of objects.

DISTANCE (FROM SUN): 4.5 to 8.2 billion km

Planet 9

Planet X

Planet 9 (also known as Planet X) is a hypothetical, large planet thought to exist in the far outer Solar System, beyond Neptune, whose gravitational attraction explains the strange, clustered orbits of numerous distant ice planets. While not yet discovered, it is thought to be 5-10 times the mass of Earth, much larger than Pluto, with an extremely eccentric orbit that takes thousands of years to circle the Sun, making it faint and difficult to locate, however recent infrared evidence reveals a possible contender.

DISTANCE (FROM SUN): 90 billion kilometers

Estimated Size: 2 to 4 times Earth's radius



OORT CLOUD

OORT CLOUD

The Oort Cloud is a vast, theoretical spherical shell of icy bodies (comets) that surrounds the Solar System and marks its outermost boundary. It was proposed by Jan Oort in 1950 and is thought to be the source of long-period comets. It is made up of trillions of frozen water, ammonia, and methane fragments that are scattered far beyond the Kuiper Belt by giant planet gravity. It is extremely distant, potentially stretching a quarter to halfway to the next star, making direct observation difficult, although its presence is deduced from comets.

DISTANCE: 2.5M LY


OUTER SPACE

OUTER SPACE

Outer space is the immense realm beyond Earth's atmosphere, a near-perfect vacuum that contains planets, stars, galaxies, and other celestial bodies. The Kármán line (about 100 km above) marks the border between air and space. It has extremely low particle density, cosmic radiation, magnetic fields, and a Big Bang-like baseline temperature. Outer space spans the entire cosmos, which is made up of dark energy, dark matter, and conventional matter, and is governed by international conventions such as the Outer Space Treaty, which encourages peaceful exploration.



NEAR BY SYSTEMS

NEAR BY SYSTEMS

ALPHA CENTAURI

Alpha Centauri is the closest star system to our Sun, located approximately 4.37 light-years away in the constellation Centaurus. It is a triple star system composed of two brilliant, Sun-like stars (Alpha Centauri A & B) and a dim red dwarf (Proxima Centauri). While Alpha Centauri A and B form a close binary pair, Proxima Centauri is the single closest star to Earth, making the system a prime target for exoplanet research, with planets confirmed around Proxima Centauri and candidates suspected around A and B, according to NASA Science and the University of Arizona News.

DISTANCE: 4.3 LY • TYPE: TRIPLE STAR SYSTEM

NEAR BY SYSTEMS

LUHMAN 16

Luhman 16 (also known as WISE 1049−5319 or WISE J104915.57−531906.1) is a binary brown-dwarf system located in the southern constellation Vela, situated 6.51 light-years (2.00 parsecs) away from the Sun. These are the nearest-known brown dwarfs and the closest system identified since the measurement of Barnard's Star's proper motion in 1916, as well as the third-closest-known system to the Sun (following the Alpha Centauri system and Barnard's Star). The primary has a spectral type of L7.5, while the secondary has a type of T0.5±1, placing it close to the L–T transition. Luhman 16 A and B have masses of 35.4 and 29.4 Jupiter masses, respectively, and are believed to have ages between 400 and 800 million years. Luhman 16 A and B revolve around one another at a separation of about 3.5 astronomical units, taking around 26.6 years for one complete orbit.

DISTANCE: 6.5 LY • TYPE: BINARY STAR

NEAR BY SYSTEMS

WOLF 359

Wolf 359 is a nearby red dwarf star, one of our closest stellar neighbors, but it's best known in science fiction for the devastating Battle of Wolf 359, a major defeat for the United Federation of Planets against the Borg Collective in Star Trek: The Next Generation, as well as a popular sci-fi podcast of the same title. The true star is a faint, tiny M-type star around 7.8 light-years away in the constellation Leo, whereas the fictitious fight resulted in a catastrophic Starfleet loss near that star system.

DISTANCE: 7.8 LY • TYPE: SINGLE RED DWARF SYSTEM

NEAR BY SYSTEMS

SIRIUS

Sirius 8.6 refers to Sirius, the brightest star in our night sky, which is around 8.6 light-years away from Earth, making it one of our nearest stellar neighbors and explaining its remarkable brilliance. This distance indicates that the light we see today departed Sirius approximately 8.6 years ago. Sirius is a binary system that includes the brilliant Sirius A and its dim white dwarf companion, Sirius B, both of which are situated in the constellation Canis Major.

DISTANCE: 8.6 LY • TYPE: BINARY STAR SYSTEM

NEAR BY SYSTEMS

TRAPPIST 1

TRAPPIST-1 is an ultra-cool red dwarf star located about 40 light-years away. It is famous for hosting a system of seven Earth-sized, rocky exoplanets, several of which orbit within the star's habitable zone, where liquid water could potentially exist, making it an ideal target for studying life beyond our solar system. This system, discovered with the TRAnsiting Planets and PlanetesImals Small Telescope (TRAPPIST), is a key target for telescopes such as the James Webb Space Telescope (JWST) to investigate planetary atmospheres.

DISTANCE: 40 LY • TYPE: RED DWARF STAR SYSTEM


MILKY WAY

HOME GALAXY

MILKY WAY

The Milky Way is a spiral galaxy containing a disk of stars that extends over 100,000 light-years. Earth is situated on one of the spiral arms of the galaxy, approximately midway to the center. Our solar system requires approximately 240 million years to complete one orbit around the Milky Way. From our viewpoint on Earth, the Milky Way appears as a dim, milky streak of light spanning the whole sky, which is the origin of its name. This attribute highlights the central disk of our home galaxy observed from the side. The Milky Way is located in an area with more than 50 other galaxies known as the Local Group. Its constituents vary in scale from dwarf galaxies (minuscule galaxies containing up to a few billion stars) to Andromeda, our closest substantial galactic neighbor. The Local Group is positioned near the boundary of the Virgo cluster and belongs to the Laniakea supercluster.

SIZE: 100,000 light-years in diameter • TYPE: LARGE SPIRAL GALAXY


ANDROMEDA

NEIGHBOURING GALAXY

ANDROMEDA

The Andromeda Galaxy is a barred spiral galaxy and is the closest significant galaxy to the Milky Way. Initially called the Andromeda Nebula, it is listed as Messier 31, M31, and NGC 224. Andromeda features a D25 isophotal diameter close to 46.56 kiloparsecs (152,000 light-years)[8] and is situated roughly 765 kpc (2.5 million light-years) away from Earth. The name of the galaxy originates from the section of the sky above Earth where it is seen, the Andromeda constellation, named after the princess who was the spouse of Perseus in Greek mythology. The virial mass of the Andromeda Galaxy is similar in scale to that of the Milky Way, estimated at 1 trillion solar masses (2.0×10^42 kilograms). Estimating the mass of either galaxy accurately is challenging, but historically it was believed that the Andromeda Galaxy had a greater mass than the Milky Way by approximately 25% to 50%. However, early-21st-century research has raised doubts about this, suggesting that the Andromeda Galaxy might have a lower mass and the Milky Way could be more massive.

SIZE: 220,000 to 260,000 light-years in diameter • TYPE: BARRED SPIRAL GALAXY


SPACE OBJECTS

Black Holes

Dark Empty Void

Black holes are regions in space with gravity so strong that nothing, not even light, can escape. They are formed from collapsed massive stars or dense matter and are characterized by a central singularity and a boundary known as the event horizon, which are invisible but detectable by their effects on surrounding matter. There are three types of black holes: stellar-mass, intermediate-mass, and supermassive, and they can be found at galactic centers such as Sagittarius A*.

SIZE : 1,500 - 1,000,000,000+ light-years

Quasars

Quasi-Stellar Objects

Quasars are extremely luminous active galactic nuclei (AGN) powered by supermassive black holes at the centers of distant galaxies. They shine brighter than their host galaxies by consuming massive amounts of surrounding gas and dust, forming superheated, spiraling accretion disks that emit enormous electromagnetic radiation, making them visible over billions of light-years and critical for studying the early universe.

SIZE : 1-2 light-years

Neutron Stars

Pulsars

Neutron stars are very dense relics of enormous stars that exploded as supernovae, packing more material than our Sun into a sphere only roughly the size of a city (10-20 kilometers). They are formed from collapsing star cores and are largely composed of neutrons. They have enormous gravity, rotate quickly, and frequently have high magnetic fields, giving them the appearance of rapidly blinking pulsars. They are extreme physics labs that test theories of gravity, density, and nuclear forces, and their density is second only to that of black holes.

RADIUS : 10 - 20 KM

Nebula

Stellar Nursery

A nebula (plural: nebulae) is a massive interstellar cloud of dust, hydrogen, helium, and other gases that act as cosmic "nurseries" for new stars or as the remains of dying stars such as supernovas. These cosmic clouds are essential to the stellar life cycle, as gravity draws material together to form stars, and dying stars scatter heavy elements back into space, enriching the universe for future generations of stars and planets. Nebulae can take many shapes, including emission, reflection, planetary, and black nebulae, and they frequently appear as magnificent, colorful structures photographed by powerful observatories like Hubble.

SIZE : few light-years to millions of light-years

Star Clusters

Stellar Clusters

A star cluster is a group of stars that are gravitationally bound together and have a common origin from the same giant cloud of gas and dust, making them similar in age and composition. There are two types: loose, younger open clusters (like the Pleiades) and dense, ancient globular clusters, which are massive spheres of older stars orbiting galactic halos. Astronomers use cluster analysis to better understand star formation and galaxy evolution.

SIZE : 17 - 300+ light years

Galaxy Clusters

Galaxy Groups

A galaxy cluster is the universe's biggest known gravitationally bound structure, encompassing hundreds to thousands of galaxies, vast amounts of superheated gas, and massive amounts of dark matter, all held together by gravity and creating the densest sections of the cosmic web. These huge systems are critical for understanding cosmic evolution and hosting giant galaxies, with dense clusters frequently including massive elliptical galaxies at their core.

SIZE : millions of light-years

Cosmic Structure

Cosmic Web / Super Cluster

Cosmic structure refers to the universe's large-scale, web-like arrangement of matter, which ranges from stars and galaxies to gigantic galaxy clusters, superclusters, filaments, and immense voids, all of which are connected by the gravity of dark matter. This cosmic web formed from minuscule density fluctuations in the early, almost uniform universe, resulting in a framework of filaments and sheets that joined galaxies and clusters separated by vast, empty supervoids, while massive formations such as the Big Ring call uniformity ideas into question.

SIZE : billions of light years

Supervoids

Cosmic Voids

Supervoids, which are enormous, mostly empty areas of space that span hundreds of millions to billions of light-years and form the enormous voids between galaxy filaments and clusters in the universe's web-like structure, are essential for comprehending cosmic evolution and the impact of dark energy. They originate from early fluctuations in matter density and grow as gravity pulls matter into denser walls.

SIZE : 100,000,000 - 1,000,000,000+ light years



THE UNIVERSE

THE EVERYTHING

THE OBSERVABLE UNIVERSE

The observable universe is the spherical area of space surrounding Earth that includes all matter whose light or signals have had sufficient time to reach us since the Big Bang, effectively forming a cosmic boundary constrained by the finite speed of light and the universe's age (approximately 13.8 billion years). It’s a sphere around Earth, having a radius of approximately 46.5 billion light-years, but it doesn’t encompass the whole universe, which could be infinite; it merely represents the part we can presently observe.
What it is:
- Cosmic Horizon: Similar to how a vessel at sea cannot perceive beyond the horizon, we are also unable to see past a specific point in space since light from those regions hasn't arrived here yet.
- Finite Age & Light's Speed: Given the universe's inception (Big Bang) and the finite speed of light, there exists a maximum distance light could have traveled to arrive at us, establishing our observable boundary.
- Expansion of Space: The universe is growing, causing far-off galaxies to drift away from us, elongating the light (redshift) and widening the gap to them, which explains why the observable universe exceeds 13.8 billion light-years.
- Not a Physical Barrier: The boundary of the observable universe isn't a barrier; it merely represents the extent of what we can perceive. Additional universe lies beyond it, but we are unable to perceive it at this time.
What Lies Within? It encompasses all galaxies, stars, and matter that we are able to detect at present, including the Cosmic Microwave Background (CMB) (the universe's oldest light). What's Lays Ahead? Anything past this horizon, which remains a section of the vast (potentially limitless) universe, is merely outside our present sight, with its light still making its way to us.

• SIZE: 93 billion light-years or potentialy endless
